    Definition:

    In the number system, different bases represent different types of numbers: base 2 is binary, base 8 is octal, base 10 is decimal, and base 16 is hexadecimal.

    • The decimal number system has a base value of 10. It produces numbers using ten digits (0–9).
    • Each digit in the number has a specific place value, which is a product of different powers of ten.
    • The place value is referred to from right to left, with the first place value being named units, the second being tens, and so on for hundreds, thousands, etc.
    • The place value for units is 10^0, tens is 10^1, hundreds is 10^2, thousands is 10^3, and so on.

    Decimal to Binary Conversion

    To convert a decimal number to binary, we follow these steps:

    1. Divide the Decimal Number by 2: Start by dividing the decimal number by 2. Note the quotient and the remainder.
    2. Repeat Division: Continue dividing the quotient by 2 until the quotient becomes 0. Each time, note the remainder.
    3. Write Remainders in Reverse Order: Once the quotient becomes 0, write down the remainders in reverse order. This gives you the binary equivalent of the decimal number.
    Steps Number System Quotient Remainder
    1 13 6 1
    2 6 3 0
    3 3 1 1
    4 1 0 1

    Reading the remainders in reverse order, we get 1101.

    So, the binary equivalent of 13 is 1101

    Decimal to Octal Conversion

    Definition:

    Octal numbers, based on 8s and using digits 0-7, can be converted to decimal by multiplying each digit by its place value and adding the results. Place values are 80, 81, and 82. Octal is handy for representing UTF-8 characters.

    Steps Number System Quotient Remainder
    1 57 7 1
    2 7 0 7

    Reading the remainders in reverse order, we get 71.

    So, the octal equivalent of 57 is 71.

    Converting with Remainders (For fractional part)

    Let's convert the fractional part of the decimal number 0.625 into binary.

    Multiply by 2:

    • Start with the fractional part (0.625) and multiply it by the base (2). This gives us 1.25. We record the integer part (1) and keep the fractional part (0.25).
    • Multiply the Fractional Part by 2 Again:
    • We take the remaining fractional part (0.25) and multiply it by 2, yielding 0.5. Again, we record the integer part (0) and keep the new fractional part (0.5).
    • Repeat:
    • We continue this process, multiplying the fractional part by 2 each time and recording the integer part until we either reach the desired precision or find a repeating pattern.
    Steps Fractional Part Integer Part Binary Digit
    1 0.625 1 1
    2 0.25 0 0
    3 0.5 1 1

    Example: Converting 0.35 to octal

    1. Multiply the decimal fraction by the base of the target system (octal, so base 8).
      • 0.35×8=2.80.35×8=2.8
    2. Write down the integer part of the result (2), and then consider only the fractional part (0.8).
    3. Repeat steps 1 and 2 with the fractional part until the fractional part becomes zero or until you have enough digits of precision.
      • 0.8×8=6.40.8×8=6.4 --> 6
      • 0.4×8=3.20.4×8=3.2 --> 3
    4. The process stops when the fractional part becomes zero or when you have enough precision for your needs. In this case, the octal representation of 0.35 is 0.26.
    Steps Fractional Part Result
    1 0.35 x 8 = 2.80 2
    2 0.8 x 8 = 6.4 6
    3 0.4 x 8 =3.2 3

    Decimal to Hexadecimal Conversion

    Hexadecimal, with a base of 16, uses digits from 0 to 9 and A to F (representing 10 to 15). It's handy for memory addresses.

    To convert a decimal number to hexadecimal:

    1. Divide the Decimal Number by 16: Start by dividing the decimal number by 16. Note the quotient and the remainder.
    2. Repeat Division: Continue dividing the quotient by 16 until the quotient becomes 0. Each time, note the remainder.
    3. Convert Remainders to Hexadecimal Digits: For remainders greater than 9, use the corresponding hexadecimal letters (A, B, C, D, E, F).
    4. Write Remainders in Reverse Order: Once the quotient becomes 0, write down the remainders in reverse order. This gives you the hexadecimal equivalent of the decimal number.
    Steps Decimal Number Quotient Remainder Hexadecimal Digit
    1 314 19 A A
    2 19 1 3 3
    3 1 0 1 1

    Reading the remainders in reverse order, we get 13A.

    So, the hexadecimal equivalent of 314 is 13A

    Division Decimals Division with decimals is similar to division with whole numbers but requires careful attention to decimal placement.

    Set up the division problem:

    $$\require{enclose} \begin{array}{r@{\hspace{0.5em}}l} 8.75 & \\ {\text ÷ 0.25} & \\ \end{array}$$

    Ignore the decimals temporarily: Pretend both numbers are whole numbers and perform the division ignoring the decimals. So, in this case, divide 875 by 25.

    $$\require{enclose} \begin{array}{r@{\hspace{0.5em}}l} 35 & \\ 25 \enclose{longdiv}{875} & \\ \underline{-75}\phantom{2} & \\ 175 & \\ \underline{-175} & \\ 0 & \\ \end{array}$$

    Bring back the decimal: Count the total number of decimal places in both the dividend and the divisor

    $$\require{enclose} \begin{array}{r@{\hspace{0.5em}}l} 8.75 & \\ {\text ÷ 0.25} & \\ \end{array}$$

    In this case, we can multiply both the dividend and divisor by 100 to move the decimal points two places to the right:

    $$\require{enclose} \begin{array}{r@{\hspace{0.5em}}l} 875 & \\ {\text ÷ 25} & \\ \end{array}$$

    Now, divide as if both numbers are whole numbers.

    $$\require{enclose} \begin{array}{r@{\hspace{0.5em}}l} 35 & \\ 25 \enclose{longdiv}{875} & \\ \underline{-75}\phantom{2} & \\ 175 & \\ \underline{-175} & \\ 0 & \\ \end{array}$$

    Count the total decimal places in both numbers, then place the decimal point in the quotient to match the difference between the decimal places in the dividend and divisor.

    $$\require{enclose} \begin{array}{r@{\hspace{0.5em}}l} {8.75 \text ÷ 0.25= 35} & \\ \end{array}$$
